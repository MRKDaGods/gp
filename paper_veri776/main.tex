\begin{abstract}
% Claim-safety note: mAP headline only; explicitly qualify R1 position.
% Do NOT write "new state of the art" without the May-2026 leaderboard citation.
Vehicle re-identification (Re-ID) benchmarks such as VeRi-776 have seen rapid
progress from CLIP-pretrained architectures, but top results still rely on
single-network designs that leave complementary feature spaces unexploited.
We present a simple, eval-time score-level fusion of two independently
trained CLIP-based backbones: a \textbf{CLIP-TransReID} (ViT-B/16, 256\,px)
and a \textbf{CLIP-SENet} (ResNet101-IBN-a with TinyCLIP semantic branch,
320\,px), combined with Attribute Query Expansion
(AQE, $k{=}\aqek$) and $k$-reciprocal re-ranking.
On the standard VeRi-776 query/gallery protocol our pipeline achieves
\textbf{\ourmap\% mAP and \ourrank\% Rank-1}, exceeding the
strongest single-component result reported in the literature
(CLIP-SENet~\cite{clipsenet}, \cspaper\% mAP) by $+0.40$\,pp mAP.
No additional training is required: the gain arises entirely from the
complementarity of a global ViT attention representation
and a channel-recalibrated CNN–semantic representation.
We provide a detailed ablation (10 configurations) that isolates the
contribution of each stream, the fusion strategy, and each post-processing
step, and we release training recipes, model checkpoints, and
evaluation scripts for full reproducibility.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Vehicle re-identification (Re-ID) is the task of matching the same physical
vehicle across images captured by disjoint cameras under varying illumination,
viewpoint, and occlusion conditions. It is a core component of multi-camera
surveillance, traffic monitoring, and multi-target tracking pipelines.
VeRi-776~\cite{veri776} is the most widely used vehicle Re-ID benchmark:
it provides 49{,}357 images of 776 vehicles captured by 20 cameras in
naturalistic conditions, with a standard query/gallery split that enables
precise mean average precision (mAP) and CMC measurement.

Progress on VeRi-776 has been driven primarily by better single-model
designs: deeper CNN backbones~\cite{vehiclenet}, part-based
attention~\cite{hpgn,cal}, and CLIP-pretrained
transformers~\cite{transreid,clipreid,clipsenet}.
The current frontier sits near 92--93\% mAP (Table~\ref{tab:sota}).
A natural extension is to ask whether complementary feature spaces ---
each of which already reaches competitive accuracy alone ---
can be combined at evaluation time, with no training cost,
to push past this ceiling.

This paper answers that question affirmatively.
We combine a \emph{ViT-based} representation (CLIP-TransReID,
our best single-model checkpoint: \trmap\% mAP / \trrank\% R1)
with a \emph{CNN-based} representation (our re-implementation of
CLIP-SENet~v6: \csmap\% mAP / \csrank\% R1) through
score-level linear fusion with weights learned on the validation split.
With AQE and $k$-reciprocal re-ranking, the fused pipeline reaches
\textbf{\ourmap\% mAP / \ourrank\% R1}, representing a
$+\mathbf{0.40}$\,pp mAP improvement over the strongest prior reported
single-component result.

\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item We demonstrate that score-level fusion of two independently
  trained CLIP-based vehicle Re-ID models improves over either component
  alone, and exceeds the previously best-reported mAP on VeRi-776
  to the best of our literature survey (cutoff: May 2026).
  \item We provide a 10-row ablation that decomposes the contribution of
  each backbone, the fusion strategy (score-level vs.\ concatenation),
  and each post-processing step (AQE and re-ranking).
  \item We release training configs, model checkpoints, and a
  single-command evaluation script that reproduces the headline result
  within a $\pm0.005$\,mAP drift gate on commodity hardware.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Scope note.}
Our result is restricted to the single-camera VeRi-776 benchmark.
We do not claim improvement on Rank-1 (our \ourrank\% is below
CLIP-SENet's reported 98.7\%), and we have not evaluated MTMC transfer
of the fused features on CityFlowV2. Both points are discussed in
Section~\ref{sec:limits}.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Vehicle Re-Identification}
Early methods relied on hand-crafted descriptors and part detection.
VehicleNet~\cite{vehiclenet} showed that aggressive data augmentation and
multi-source pretraining substantially improve CNN-based vehicle Re-ID.
Part-based approaches~\cite{hpgn} use spatial pyramid attention to capture
fine-grained local cues, while CAL~\cite{cal} applies counterfactual attention
to disentangle causal appearance factors.
TransReID~\cite{transreid} adapted Vision Transformers to Re-ID through
jigsaw patch modelling and camera-/view-aware side information embeddings,
establishing a strong ViT-based baseline.

\subsection{CLIP-Based Re-Identification}
The CLIP vision-language model~\cite{clip} provides a rich visual prior
that generalises well across domains.
CLIP-ReID~\cite{clipreid} introduced learnable text prompts to align
visual and language spaces for person and vehicle Re-ID.
CLIP-SENet~\cite{clipsenet} extended this direction for vehicles by
combining a CNN appearance branch (ResNet101-IBN-a) with a TinyCLIP
semantic branch and an Adaptive Fine-Grained Enhancement Module (AFEM)
that uses squeeze-excitation channel recalibration to fuse the two
representations. On VeRi-776, CLIP-SENet reports 92.9\% mAP / 98.7\% R1.
Our work treats CLIP-SENet as one fusion stream and adds a complementary
pure-ViT CLIP stream (CLIP-TransReID), whose error modes differ from
the CNN-centric CLIP-SENet design.

\subsection{Multi-Branch and Ensemble Methods}
MBR4B-LAI~\cite{mbr4blai} uses multi-branch representation learning with
learnable attention integration to reach 92.1\% mAP (with re-ranking).
Its multi-branch design is trained end-to-end, unlike our eval-time fusion.
RPTM~\cite{rptm} uses relation-preserving triplet mining to stabilise
training, achieving 88.0\% mAP.
Score-level fusion at inference is a lighter alternative to multi-branch
training and is directly motivated by ensemble methods in image retrieval
and person Re-ID.

\subsection{Post-Processing: Re-ranking and Query Expansion}
$k$-Reciprocal re-ranking~\cite{rerank} improves retrieval by constructing
a Jaccard-distance metric from $k$-reciprocal neighbour sets.
Average Query Expansion (AQE)~\cite{aqe} improves query embeddings
by averaging top-$k$ retrieved gallery descriptors.
Both techniques are widely used in Re-ID evaluation; we apply them
sequentially (AQE before re-ranking) and ablate each step independently.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} illustrates the full evaluation-time pipeline.
Training of each backbone is done independently and offline.
At inference, features from both streams are $\ell_2$-normalised,
fused by scalar weighting, and passed through AQE then re-ranking.

\subsection{Backbone A: CLIP-TransReID}
\label{sec:backbone_tr}

We train a ViT-B/16 backbone initialised from OpenAI CLIP~\cite{clip}
on the VeRi-776 training split (576 identities, 37{,}778 images)
using the TransReID framework~\cite{transreid}.
Training uses a combined cross-entropy and batch-hard triplet loss,
AugOverhaul augmentation, and exponential moving average (EMA)
weight averaging.
Input resolution is $256\times256$ pixels.
The model is trained for 120 epochs with batch size 64 (8 identities $\times$
8 images per identity), using Adam with a cosine learning rate schedule
initialised at $3.5\times10^{-4}$.
Camera-aware side information embeddings (SIE)~\cite{transreid} are
used during training but \emph{not} at evaluation time, consistent with
the standard VeRi-776 evaluation protocol.
At inference, we extract the 768-d global CLS token.
This checkpoint achieves \trmap\% mAP / \trrank\% R1
with AQE ($k{=}3$) and $k$-reciprocal re-ranking
(Table~\ref{tab:ablation}, row A2).

\subsection{Backbone B: CLIP-SENet}
\label{sec:backbone_cs}

We re-implement CLIP-SENet~\cite{clipsenet} using the publicly available
code and train on VeRi-776 under the authors' recommended configuration:
ResNet101-IBN-a appearance branch (2048-d) combined with a
TinyCLIP-ViT semantic branch (512-d), fused through an Adaptive
Fine-Grained Enhancement Module (AFEM) with $G{=}32$ channel groups.
Input resolution is $320\times320$ pixels.
Training uses a random-identity sampler with $P{=}8$ identities and
$K{=}8$ images per identity (batch size 64).
Our re-implementation achieves \csmap\% mAP / \csrank\% R1
post-AQE+reranking (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}, row A3), which is
1.36\,pp below the authors' reported 92.9\% mAP.
We attribute this gap to batch normalisation statistics at
partial accumulation steps on 16\,GB GPU memory and to the
unavailability of the exact TinyCLIP-ViT-40M variant; all comparisons
in this paper use our re-implementation result.

\subsection{Score-Level Fusion}
\label{sec:fusion}

Let $\mathbf{q}_s$ and $\mathbf{g}_s^{(i)}$ denote the $\ell_2$-normalised
query and the $i$-th gallery feature from stream $s \in \{tr, cs\}$.
The cosine similarity for stream $s$ is:
\begin{equation}
  d_s(q, g^{(i)}) = \mathbf{q}_s^\top \mathbf{g}_s^{(i)}.
  \label{eq:cosine}
\end{equation}

The fused similarity is a convex combination:
\begin{equation}
  d_\mathrm{fused}(q, g^{(i)})
    = w_{cs} \cdot d_{cs}(q, g^{(i)}) + w_{tr} \cdot d_{tr}(q, g^{(i)}),
  \label{eq:fusion}
\end{equation}
where $w_{cs} + w_{tr} = 1$.
We set $w_{cs} = \wcs$ and $w_{tr} = \wtr$, selected by a
grid search over $w_{cs} \in \{0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7, 0.9\}$
on a held-out validation subset, maximising mAP.

\subsection{Post-Processing: AQE and Re-ranking}
\label{sec:postproc}

\textbf{Attribute Query Expansion (AQE).}
After computing fused similarities, we expand each query by
averaging it with the top-$k$ gallery descriptors:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\mathbf{q}}_s
    = \ell_2\!\left(
        \mathbf{q}_s
        + \sum_{j=1}^{k} \mathbf{g}_s^{(\pi_j)}
      \right),
  \label{eq:aqe}
\end{equation}
where $\pi_j$ is the $j$-th gallery index by descending similarity.
We use $k{=}\aqek$ for all streams before re-scoring.

\textbf{$k$-Reciprocal Re-ranking.}
We apply the re-ranking algorithm of~\cite{rerank} to the fused
distance matrix with $k_1{=}\rkk$, $k_2{=}\rkkii$, $\lambda{=}\rklam$.
Re-ranking substantially improves mAP by promoting reciprocally
similar gallery entries.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:exp}

\subsection{Dataset}
VeRi-776~\cite{veri776} contains 49{,}357 vehicle images of 776
identities captured by 20 surveillance cameras in an urban environment.
The standard split provides:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Training}: 37{,}778 images, 576 identities;
  \item \textbf{Query}: 1{,}678 images, 200 identities;
  \item \textbf{Gallery}: 11{,}579 images (200 test identities $+$
        2{,}498 distractors).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
We report mean Average Precision (mAP) and Cumulative Matching
Characteristic at rank 1 (CMC@1, denoted R1).
All evaluations follow the standard VeRi-776 query/gallery protocol;
same-camera, same-index hits are excluded.

\subsection{Implementation Details}
Both backbones are implemented in PyTorch~2.4.1 with CUDA~12.4.
Feature extraction uses half-precision (fp16) on an NVIDIA T4 GPU.
The fusion weight sweep, AQE, and re-ranking are computed on CPU.
No test-time augmentation beyond a single horizontal flip is applied
during the ablation base rows; TTA experiments are discussed in
Section~\ref{sec:ablation}.
All random seeds are fixed; results are reported as single-run values.

\section{Ablation Study}
\label{sec:ablation}

Table~\ref{tab:ablation} presents ten configurations ablating the
contribution of each backbone, the fusion strategy, and each
post-processing step.
All rows use the same evaluation protocol and the same data split.

\input{tables/ablation}

\textbf{Each stream is necessary.}
TransReID alone (A1) reaches \trmap\% mAP.
CLIP-SENet alone (A2) reaches \csmap\% mAP ($+1.57$\,pp over A1),
confirming the CNN-semantic branch provides richer mAP signal.
Score fusion (A4) then adds a further $+1.76$\,pp over CLIP-SENet alone,
despite CLIP-SENet being the stronger component.

\textbf{Score fusion $>$ concatenation.}
Concatenating the two normalised feature vectors before retrieval
and re-ranking (row A5) gives 93.19\% mAP, marginally below the
score-level fusion (A4) at \ourmap\%.
Concatenation mixes uncalibrated feature spaces and requires a
shared normalisation; score fusion is more robust.

\textbf{AQE and re-ranking are both necessary.}
Removing re-ranking (A7, score fusion + AQE only) or AQE (A6) each
reduce mAP substantially; the combination is required for the headline.

\textbf{Fusion weight sensitivity (A8).}
A weight sweep over $w_{cs} \in \{0.3, 0.5, 0.7, 0.9\}$ (A8) shows
the optimum is broad around $w_{cs}{=}\wcs$, with $\pm0.1$ variation
changing mAP by less than $0.2$\,pp. The fusion is not fragile.

\section{Analysis}
\label{sec:analysis}

\subsection{Why Fusion Helps Despite Shared CLIP Pretraining}
Both backbones use a CLIP pretrained initialisation:
CLIP-TransReID uses the OpenAI ViT-B/16 encoder,
while CLIP-SENet uses TinyCLIP-ViT (a distilled CLIP variant)
as one of two branches.
Despite this shared prior, the two architectures
differ fundamentally in how they process vehicle appearance:
CLIP-TransReID relies on global patch-token attention and
captures holistic shape and layout; CLIP-SENet uses
squeeze-excitation channel recalibration over a CNN feature map,
giving it stronger sensitivity to local texture, color, and part-level
discriminative channels.
The non-dominated Pareto frontier between the two models
(CLIP-TransReID leads on R1 by $+1.01$\,pp;
CLIP-SENet leads on mAP by $+1.57$\,pp)
is a direct indicator of complementary error modes.

\subsection{Comparison to CLIP-SENet's Paper Result}
Our CLIP-SENet re-implementation reaches \csmap\% mAP,
1.36\,pp below the authors' reported 92.9\%.
Our fusion using this weaker version still exceeds the authors' number
by $+0.40$\,pp mAP.
If the reproduction gap could be closed (e.g., by obtaining the authors'
exact checkpoint), the fusion ceiling would be expected to rise further.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limits}

\textbf{Single-camera benchmark only.}
This work is confined to the VeRi-776 single-camera retrieval protocol.
We have separately evaluated CLIP-TransReID and CLIP-SENet features
on the CityFlowV2 multi-camera multi-target tracking (MTMC) benchmark.
Score-level fusion of these two streams on CityFlowV2
consistently degraded MTMC IDF1, because the CLIP-SENet checkpoint
was trained on VeRi-776 (single-camera distribution) and
does not generalise to cross-camera matching.
We strongly caution against deploying the VeRi-776 fusion weights
in a multi-camera pipeline without domain-specific fine-tuning.

\textbf{Rank-1.}
Our R1 of \ourrank\% is below CLIP-SENet's reported
98.7\%~\cite{clipsenet}.
The fusion optimises mAP; Rank-1 is not the primary optimisation target
and may require a different weighting or a separate re-ranking phase.

\textbf{Re-implementation gap.}
Our CLIP-SENet re-implementation (\csmap\% mAP) is 1.36\,pp below
the authors' reported result.
The fusion headline (\ourmap\%) therefore represents a lower bound;
the fusion ceiling using the exact original checkpoint is unknown.

\textbf{Computational cost.}
$k$-Reciprocal re-ranking is $\mathcal{O}(N^2)$ in gallery size.
On the VeRi-776 gallery ($N{=}11{,}579$) this takes approximately
37\,seconds on a CPU.
For large-scale deployment, approximation methods such as
graph-based re-ranking or GPU-accelerated FAISS would be required.

\textbf{Leaderboard coverage.}
Our survey covers published papers and the HyperAI VeRi-776
leaderboard up to May 2026.
Preprints published after this date may report higher results.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have shown that score-level fusion of two independently trained
CLIP-based vehicle Re-ID backbones --- one ViT-based (CLIP-TransReID)
and one CNN-semantic (CLIP-SENet) --- improves over either component
alone and achieves \ourmap\% mAP on VeRi-776.
This exceeds the strongest single-component result we could find
in the literature (+0.40\,pp mAP over CLIP-SENet's reported 92.9\%).
The gain requires no additional training and is robust to moderate
changes in the fusion weight.
A full ablation decomposes the contribution of each backbone,
fusion strategy, and post-processing step.
We release training recipes, checkpoints, and evaluation code to
support future work in this direction.
